## 1. The 4 Laws of Job Hunting

The mindset foundation. Get this right before everything else.

### Law 1 — The Fog (Get Wet, But You'll Get There)

The path forward is never perfectly clear. You won't see the whole staircase — just the next step. Stop waiting for clarity before you move. Walk into the fog anyway. Getting uncomfortable, confused, and uncertain *is* the process. You will get there.

### Law 2 — Perception Is Reality (Put Your Best Foot Forward)

How you show up matters as much as your actual skills. Your resume, your LinkedIn, your GitHub, the way you carry yourself in an interview — these are the signals people use to form judgments. Control the narrative. Perception *is* reality until proven otherwise, so make sure the reality you're projecting is the one you want.

### Law 3 — Don't Be a Jr. (Imposter Syndrome — Don't Label Yourself Average)

Stop labeling yourself "junior." Don't apply to junior roles if you don't have to. The label creates a ceiling before you've even started. Every developer who got hired senior once felt like they didn't belong. Imposter syndrome is universal — don't let it define your job title. Carry yourself like the engineer you're becoming, not the one you were.

### Law 4 — Are You Swimming with Everyone Else?

If you follow the exact same process as every other candidate — mass-applying on LinkedIn, sending the same resume template — you'll get the same results: silence. Standing out requires doing something different. The candidates who get noticed are the ones who took an unexpected angle.

## 2. Is This Career Right for Me?

Don't skip this step. The most expensive mistake is chasing the wrong destination.

**Required Reading: *So Good They Can't Ignore You* by Cal Newport**

Stop following your passion. Instead, build rare and valuable skills, then trade them for work you love. Passion follows mastery — not the other way around. Read this before you commit to a direction.

**Steps:**

1. Explore career paths with intention — use a structured map to understand what skills are needed at each level. Start at [zerotomastery.io/career-paths](https://zerotomastery.io/career-paths/).
2. Ask yourself the hard question daily: *"If today was the last day of my life, would I want to do what I'm about to do today?"*
3. Read Ecclesiastes. Perspective check. The clearest thinking about meaning, work, and what actually matters — see the [Ecclesiastes & Mortality Framework](#ecclesiastes--the-mortality-framework) at the end of this guide.

## 3. Skill Stacking

Don't just learn skills. Stack them so the combination makes you rare.

### The Talent Stack Principle

Being world-class at one thing is nearly impossible. But combining multiple good skills in a unique way can make you extraordinary. A developer who can also communicate, write, and understand business is worth far more than a developer alone.

### Step-by-Step

**Step 1 — Identify Your Career Path**
Use [zerotomastery.io/career-paths](https://zerotomastery.io/career-paths/) to map out the concrete skills required for your target role. Don't wander — follow a curated path.

**Step 2 — Build Your Talent Stack**
Layer adjacent skills on top of your core skill. Example: DevOps engineer + Python automation + AI/Agentforce = uniquely positioned for the next wave of release management.

**Step 3 — Develop Skills Every Single Day**
Non-negotiable. Learn something new every day. The compounding effect of daily skill-building over a year is staggering. Treat it like going to the gym — skip a day if you must, but never miss two in a row.

**Step 4 — Become a Star**
Don't just be competent. Be excellent enough that people talk about you. Build projects that make people say "wow." Write. Share. Teach. Visibility amplifies skill.

## 4. Becoming a Star

Your online presence is your 24/7 silent recruiter. Make it work for you.

> Overall effort to set things up before you start applying should be **less than one week**. Don't spend too much time editing your resume — set up your presence and start applying.

### Portfolio

Helps you stand out. Paints a better picture of the type of worker you are (Perception vs. Reality).

- Have **1–2 really big projects** in your portfolio
- Use [zerotomastery.io](https://zerotomastery.io) for project ideas
- Use [GitHub Pages Quickstart](https://docs.github.com/en/pages/quickstart) to create your portfolio site
- No online presence yet? Start at [profileme.dev](https://www.profileme.dev/)
- Prompt to use: *"Can you give me a cool HTML template that is modern and can list my future portfolio projects"*

### Resume

- Don't over-edit. ATS-compliant, clean, and targeted.
- Customize it for roles you actually care about.

### LinkedIn

## 5. Getting More Interviews

### The Core Rule: Quality Over Quantity

**No shotgun approach.** Sending out 100 resumes is lazy and ineffective.

Use a **targeted approach: 5 companies at a time, 1 week at a time.**

- Email employees and HR people directly
- Try to get in the door for an interview, not just submit a form
- Apply locally when possible — local companies have fewer applicants

### Where to Find Jobs

**Favorites:**

- [LinkedIn Jobs](https://linkedin.com/jobs)
- [Hacker News — Who's Hiring](https://news.ycombinator.com) — Search Google: `askhn whos hiring` (search for the most recent month)
- [WellFound / AngelList](https://wellfound.com) — for startups
- [We Work Remotely](https://weworkremotely.com)
- [RemoteOK](https://remoteok.com)

**Other:**

- Google search: `site:linkedin.com facebook recruiter`
- [ZipRecruiter](https://ziprecruiter.com)
- [Meetup.com](https://meetup.com) — local networking, hidden opportunities

### Cold Email Strategy

The best way to get interviews is through **referrals**. Skip the resume whenever possible — ask to talk, ask to meet, ask how you can solve their problem.

**Chrome Extensions for finding emails:**
- Hunter
- ContactOut
- Email Extractor

**Email Template:**

```
Hey $NAME,

I saw your presentation at $CONFERENCE last year on YouTube.
Great stuff — loved what you did with $FOO, in particular
$COMMENT_PROVING_YOU_KNOW_WHAT_YOURE_TALKING_ABOUT.

I'm also a $FOO developer. I noticed your company is hiring
for $ROLE. I'd love to be a part of your team. Do you have a
few minutes to chat on Thursday about what you're building?

Thanks,
Your Name
yourwebsite.com
```

### Cover Letters

- Think **quality over quantity** — send 5–10 personalized letters
- Do your homework. Know their company's needs from the job description
- Match your unique competencies to what they're actually looking for
- Never send a generic letter

### Unique Strategies That Actually Work

- **Google everything about the company** — be able to say "I was inspired by..."
- **Attend company events**
- **Talk to employees** — "After having coffee with [name], this is where I need to be"
- **Find their tech stack** — use [stackshare.io](https://stackshare.io) and customize your approach
- **Identify and solve one of their problems** — show up with an observation
- **Contribute to open source first** — find a good first issue at [goodfirstissue.dev](https://goodfirstissue.dev), submit a PR, reference it in outreach
- **Show up unannounced** — bold, but memorable for the right company
- **Freelance as a cheat code** — use [Upwork](https://upwork.com) to build real experience

**AI Prompt to use:**
> *"I want to apply to [company] in [state]. What's the best way to apply and stand out from the crowd and be creative about it?"*

## 6. The Interview — Non-Technical

> Think of the interview as a date. You're both deciding if this is a good fit.

### Your 4 Hero Stories

Have these prepared before any interview. These are your go-to stories.

| Story | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **Technical Story** | A real problem you solved — this is where your projects come in |
| **Success Story** | Shows you are a successful person, not just a successful employee |
| **Leadership Story** | How you directed, influenced, or carried others |
| **Challenge Story** | What challenged you and how you overcame it |

### Mindset Going In

- Use phrases like **"I'm looking for a good mutual fit"** — it shifts the power dynamic
- Read about **Mirroring** and **The Chameleon Effect** — rapport-building techniques
- Get in the mindset that the interviewer is your friend, not a judge
- Tell them you're not a "know-it-all" and are always willing to collaborate
- **Always appear confident**
- Always be a shining ray of sunshine — energy is contagious

### Key Questions — How to Answer

#### "Tell me about yourself"

3 things to cover:
1. Your triggers of success
2. Skills relevant to this role (mention things you want to get asked about)
3. Answer in under a minute — tell your hero's journey

**Example (no prior experience):**

> "A few years back I got really interested in coding when I started a little side business. I'd hire developers and it was always a black box — I'd pay money and get back a product I was never fully satisfied with. So I started teaching myself to code because I wanted to understand how that black box works. The more I learned, the more engulfed I became. I started building projects, writing blog posts about technical subjects that were received really well. Through these projects, every day I'm becoming a better coder. To the point that now I feel very confident about being a valuable asset to your team — not just now, but a year from now, where I expect my skills to grow exponentially."

#### "Why do you want to work here?"

2 things to show:
1. You want to grow
2. You are the best fit

> "I've been researching your company for a while and I noticed you're working on [X] and I've done similar work in the past... I think the problem of [X] is interesting and I think this is something I can add value to and also learn from."

The key: make them feel *chosen*. You specifically researched them and this is the one.

#### "Why did you leave your last job?"

- No negativity. Always be positive.
- Tell them you wanted to grow.

#### "Tell me about a problem and how you solved it"

Use the **SAR Method:**

- **S — Situation:** Set the scene
- **A — Action:** What you specifically did
- **R — Result:** What happened — with numbers if possible

Have metrics ready. Touch on scaling, performance, or security.

*Variation of this question:* "Tell me about an interesting project?"

#### "What is your biggest weakness?"

Two parts:
1. Give a **real** answer (not "I work too hard")
2. Show how you improved it

**Example:**
> "Sometimes when there's a problem, I get straight to coding without thinking it through first. I realized this was slowing me down, so now I focus on writing pseudo-code and commenting out my assumptions before writing a single line."

### Secret Weapons — Work These Into Conversation

- **Simplicity over complexity** — "I always favor the simplest solution that solves the problem." Shows what kind of engineer you are.
- **Premature optimization is the root of all evil** — Show you understand time, resources, and tradeoffs. Is a 1% performance improvement worth the effort right now?
- **Overall goal, not myopic** — Show you focus on what the client actually needs, not just the narrow technical task in front of you.
- **No ego** — Let your work ethic speak. No complaining about codebases, clients, or colleagues. Convey that you're comfortable talking to clients and not a complainer.

### Questions to Ask the Interviewer

Focus on *them*, not the company. They're human too.

- What's the greatest mistake you made in your first month, and what did you do?
- Where do you see this company going in the next 3–5 years?
- Why did you personally join this company?
- Have you seen your own skills grow in the past year?
- Have you ever messed up, and how did the company handle it?
- **What do you wish someone had told you when you first started here?** ⭐

Additional resource: [github.com/viraptor/reverse-interview](https://github.com/RCola22/reverse-interview)

### After the Interview

- **Send a follow-up note** thanking them for their time and reaffirming your interest
- Address any questions you stumbled on during the interview
- This small gesture goes a long way — almost nobody does it
- End every interview on a strong note: why they should hire *you*
- Think: *"If they hire me, how could I make their job easier?"* Be that candidate.

### The Most Important Mindset Shift

> "Don't hope to get hired at all. Treat every interview as a learning experience."

This detachment removes panic, reduces anxiety, and ironically makes you perform better. Rejection doesn't go on your resume. It doesn't go on your profile. It just disappears. You are valuable. This interview is not do-or-die.

## 7. The Technical Interview

### Pre-Interview Meditation

Before every interview, do a short meditation. Keep one bookmarked and use it. Your mental state going in is a variable you can control — use it.

### Checklist

- [ ] Have your **Technical Hero Story** prepared with real project examples
- [ ] Know your metrics — numbers, scale, performance improvements
- [ ] Practice explaining complex topics simply
- [ ] Be honest when you don't know something — *"I haven't worked with X, but here's how I'd approach learning it..."*
- [ ] Use white-boarding to show your thinking process, not just your answer

## 8. Offers & Negotiation

> **Always negotiate.** The worst they can say is no — and most people never even ask.

### Salary Question — Use Anchoring

> "I know the average [role] salary range is [$$$] per year, so I think that's a good place for us to start."

You're starting the conversation, not ending it. Don't give a specific number first. Let them move toward your anchor. Always ask for **$10,000 more than your actual target.**

**Salary Research:**

- [Salary.com](https://salary.com)
- [Payscale.com](https://payscale.com)
- [Glassdoor.com](https://glassdoor.com)

### When They Give You a Specific Number

> "Thanks for the generous offer. However, this is a big family decision and I'm looking to work for a company for many years, so I'd like to discuss this before making any final decisions."

Give a reason for everything — this way you don't sound greedy.

### When You Receive an Offer

1. **Don't end the conversation — keep it going**
2. Be positive and express genuine excitement
3. **Ask for more time**
4. **Let other companies know you have an offer (your stake)**

**Script to accelerate competing companies:**

> "I just wanted to give you an update on my interviewing process. I've just received an offer from [company] which is quite strong. With that said, I'm really excited to potentially work with your company and I wanted to see if we can make things work. Since my timeline is a bit compressed with this new offer — is there anything we can do to move the process a bit faster?"

### Handling Multiple Offers

This is the goal state. When you have multiple offers, ask yourself (most to least important):

1. Is there an offer where I feel unqualified? → **Take it.** You're taking the job to grow.
2. Where is the greatest **long-term growth potential**?
3. Will I respect the people around me?
4. What are the salary and benefits (including bonuses, stock, relocation)?
5. Am I deciding from **desperation**? → Don't.
6. Will I be in the same position two years from now? → Don't pick the easy job.

### Handling Rejection

- Rejection doesn't go on your resume. It doesn't go on your profile. It disappears.
- Rejections are never a prediction of success.
- Always ask the interviewer for feedback.
- Always ask when it's okay to reapply.

### Getting a Raise

**Step 1 — Just ask.** If you never ask, the answer is always no.

**Step 2 — Show, don't tell.** Write a one-page summary or email to your manager with proof you're driving the metrics that matter to them.

**Start from Day One — keep a folder of:**
- Problems you solved and money you saved
- Great feedback from clients and co-workers
- Your skill progression from month 1 to month 6
- What you plan to accomplish in the next six months

## Ecclesiastes & The Mortality Framework

The daily question — *"If today was the last day of my life, would I want to do what I'm about to do today?"* — isn't just motivational language. It maps directly onto some of the oldest and clearest thinking about urgency, meaning, and action.

| Concept | Steve Jobs Quote | Ecclesiastes Philosophy |
|---|---|---|
| The Catalyst | "Remembering you will be dead soon" | "Death is the destiny of everyone" (7:2) |
| The Action | "Make the biggest choices" | "Do it with all your might" (9:10) |
| The Result | Avoiding the trap of thinking you have something to lose | Finding meaning in a "vapor-like" (hevel) existence |

### 1. The Call to Urgent Action — Ecclesiastes 9:10

Perhaps the most direct alignment. It argues that because the grave offers no opportunity for work or thought, you must act with intensity right now.

> *"Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with all your might, for in the realm of the dead, where you are going, there is neither working nor planning nor knowledge nor wisdom."*

**The Connection:** Like the Jobs quote, this verse uses the finality of death as a catalyst. It suggests that "big choices" shouldn't be met with hesitation, but with total commitment — because our window of opportunity is closing.

### 2. The Perspective Shift — Ecclesiastes 7:2–4

Counter-intuitive. Contemplating death makes you wiser than chasing distractions.

> *"It is better to go to a house of mourning than to go to a house of feasting, for death is the destiny of everyone; the living should take this to heart... The heart of the wise is in the house of mourning, but the heart of fools is in the house of pleasure."*

**The Connection:** Taking death to heart shifts your perspective. When you are in the "house of mourning," the trivialities of life fall away — allowing you to focus on the choices that actually matter.

### 3. The "Seize the Day" Command — Ecclesiastes 11:9–12:1

The Teacher encourages following your heart and eyes, with the sobering reminder that judgment and old age are coming.

> *"Be happy, young person, while you are young... follow the ways of your heart and whatever your eyes see... Remember your Creator in the days of your youth, before the days of trouble come."*

**The Connection:** Use mortality as a compass. Live authentically and make choices based on your heart's desires — but do so with the sobering filter of knowing your time is limited.
